Way to make ashes from grapevine tendrils One can these, by a pure heart, without mixing other ashes in, that are newly burnt, one puts it on the fire and the ashes all together in a bad/old earthenware pot, which one from time to time pokes, until it is all consumed. To make lye from grapevine-tendril ash Take three handfuls of ashes [scooped] with two hands, and 7 or 8 pints of rain or fountain water, let this cook until [it reduces to] 4 or 5 pints, should be cooked in a clean [metal] marmite. Once it is removed from the fire, it must be cast [i.e., levigated] into a metal pot that has not been used for any other thing, such as dyeing red, leaving the ashes, so much as possible, on the bottom of the marmite. The next day, one pours the ashes through a cloth into another pot, still careful not to move [what is on] the bottom. The lye is then good to use to dye a pink color, as follows here. Take a quarter or half pound of safflower, put it in a little white linen bag, securely tied, place it to soak in rain or fountain water. One washes(?) the safflower a little itself, changing the water two times, and then letting [it] soak from five-thirty in the evening until five-thirty in the morning. And then is it washed, and from time to time, its water is changed until it is clear. The bag should not be wrung out but removed from the water with the hands, except for the last time: then, the little bag must be very firmly wrung out, as much as is possible.
